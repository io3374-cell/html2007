The early internet was a wild and creative space. People could build their own websites, borrow bits of code from others, and make something totally unique. 
It wasn’t polished or perfect, but that was the point. It was personal, messy, and full of strange experiments. Reweirding the internet means bringing back 
that freedom to play and create without worrying about fitting into a clean template. Making this site from scratch feels like a throwback to those early days.
It reminds me of websites we explored in class like My Boyfriend Came Back from the War by Olia Lialina, which used simple HTML to tell a fragmented story. 
In class, we talked about how people used to copy parts of websites and remix them. That kind of openness made the web feel like a shared playground.
Today, most websites look the same and are built with tools that limit creativity. Reweirding is about breaking out of that and making space for glitchy, handmade, 
and unexpected designs. Trevor Paglen’s essay Invisible Images helped me think about this differently. He writes, “The relationship between images and power in 
the machine-machine landscape is different than in the human visual landscape.” That line stuck with me. It shows how the internet has changed from a place for 
people to express themselves to a space where machines talk to machines, often for surveillance or control. Reweirding is a way to push back and make the internet 
feel human again. So here's Kungfu Panda:
